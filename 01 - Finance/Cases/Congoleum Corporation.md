## 🕰️ Background

In 1979, Congoleum Corporation (a flooring manufacturer) proposed one of the first modern leveraged buyouts. The management team and PE firm First Boston proposed taking the company private at a premium to the public share price. The board had to evaluate whether the offer price was fair to shareholders.

---

## ❓ The Central Problem

The case focuses on the fairness opinion process — how investment banks determine whether a proposed transaction price is "fair" to shareholders. Students must build a DCF model and comparable analysis to assess whether the offered price represents fair value or whether management is self-dealing at an artificially low price.

---

## 📊 Analysis

Key issues: (1) Management has information asymmetry — they know the company better than outside shareholders, (2) The fairness opinion from an independent bank must weigh DCF intrinsic value, trading comps, and precedent transactions, (3) The board must balance fiduciary duty to maximize shareholder value against management's self-interest in a lower price, (4) Leveraged structure — how does adding debt change the equity required and therefore the offer price?

---

## 🔑 Key Lessons

1. **Fairness opinions are critical governance tools but depend on the assumptions used** — they can be influenced
2. Management buyouts inherently create conflicts that require independent oversight
3. **The MBO structure was a precursor to the modern PE industry** — Congoleum predated RJR by 9 years
4. Board committees must ensure independent valuation that protects minority shareholders

---

## 🎓 Discussion Questions

1. Is the offered price fair? Build a DCF and comps to determine a fair range.
2. What conflicts of interest exist when management is both buyer and the party being evaluated?
3. How should the board structure the process to protect shareholders?
