# 📚 Dell Leverage Recapitalization

> **Core Lesson**: Share buybacks, capital structure

## 🕰️ Background

In 2012, Dell Inc. faced declining PC demand and a strategic pivot to enterprise services. Michael Dell proposed taking the company private in a $24.4B leveraged buyout — the largest technology LBO in history. Activist investor Carl Icahn opposed the deal, arguing the price was too low and that Dell should instead do a leveraged recapitalization: take on debt and pay a massive special dividend to shareholders.

---

## ❓ The Central Problem

Dell LBO versus leverage recapitalization: Which creates more value for shareholders? Key questions: (1) Is Michael Dell's $13.65/share offer fair? (2) Would a leveraged recap (borrow $5-9B and distribute to shareholders) be better? (3) What's Dell's standalone value under different transformation scenarios? (4) How do you value a company mid-transition from hardware to services?

---

## 📊 Analysis

Icahn's argument: Dell's free cash flow could service additional debt, and paying a special dividend of $12-14/share would instantly return value while keeping the stock public. Dell's argument: the public market was undervaluing the company because Wall Street was focused on declining PC revenue, not the enterprise services transformation. Going private gives management time to restructure without quarterly earnings pressure. The board commissioned a go-shop process; no higher bid emerged.

---

## 🔑 Key Lessons

1. Going-private transactions give management strategic flexibility but create conflicts with public shareholders
2. **Leveraged recapitalizations are an alternative to buyouts** — they return cash without full deleveraging risk
3. **Activist investors serve as a market check on management proposals** — Icahn forced a higher price
4. Valuing companies in transition requires scenario analysis, not steady-state DCF

---

## 🎓 Discussion Questions

1. Is Dell worth more as a public company doing a levered recap or as a private company under Michael Dell?
2. How does the conflict between Dell (buyer) and Dell (CEO) create governance challenges?
3. What does Icahn's intervention tell us about the role of activist investors?
