## 🕰️ Background

Slack originated as an internal tool for a gaming company called Tiny Speck, which was developing an MMORPG titled 'Glitch.' When Glitch failed to gain traction, the founders (led by Stewart Butterfield) realized the chat tool they had built to coordinate their distributed team was better than any commercial alternative. They shut down the game and pivoted to Slack in 2013.

---

## ❓ The Central Problem

How do founders recognize when the 'side project' is more valuable than the core business? Tiny Speck had raised $17M for a game that wasn't working. The pivot to Slack required abandoning years of work to focus on a communication tool in a crowded market dominated by email and HipChat.

---

## 📊 Analysis

Slack's success came from: (1) Integrating of third-party tools (GitHub, Trello, etc.) into the chat flow, (2) User experience (the 'butter' feel—searchable, fun, emojis), (3) Bottom-up adoption (teams used it for free until centralized IT was forced to buy it), (4) Narrative: 'where work happens.' Salesforce eventually acquired Slack for $27.7B in 2021.

---

## 🔑 Key Lessons

1. Pivoting requires the courage to kill your 'baby' (the core product) to save the company
2. Internal tools often solve real problems better than external products because they are built out of necessity
3. Integrations and interoperability are competitive moats in the enterprise SaaS space
4. Bottom-up adoption (PLG - Product Led Growth) bypasses the traditional, slow B2B sales cycle

---

## 🎓 Discussion Questions

1. What signals should a founder look for before deciding to pivot?
2. How did Slack differentiate itself from entrenched incumbents like Microsoft Lync or email?
3. Was the Salesforce acquisition a success for Slack's product vision?
