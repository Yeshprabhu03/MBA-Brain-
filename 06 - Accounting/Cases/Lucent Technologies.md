# 📚 Lucent Technologies

## 🕰️ Background

Lucent Technologies (spun off from AT&T in 1996) became a Wall Street darling in the late 1990s telecom boom. Lucent used vendor financing — lending money to its own customers to buy Lucent equipment — to inflate revenue. When the telecom bubble burst, customers defaulted on their Lucent loans, revealing that revenue had been artificially supported by Lucent's own lending. Write-offs exceeded $5B.

---

## ❓ The Central Problem

How does vendor financing (lending to your own customers to buy your products) distort revenue and create hidden credit risk? Lucent's case shows that revenue quality depends not just on when it's recognized, but on whether the customer can actually pay.

---

## 📊 Analysis

Lucent's vendor financing cycle: (1) Lend $1B to a telecom startup, (2) Startup buys $1B in Lucent equipment, (3) Lucent records $1B in revenue, (4) Analyst sees revenue growth and applauds. Hidden risk: if the borrower defaults (many did in 2001-2002), the equipment sale reverses but the loan write-off stays. Lucent was effectively subsidizing its own revenue growth through credit risk. The balance sheet showed growing 'customer finance receivables' — the red flag no one read.

---

## 🔑 Key Lessons

1. Vendor financing inflates revenue by transferring credit risk to the balance sheet — revenue looks real but depends on customer solvency
2. Growing 'customer finance receivables' or 'notes receivable' relative to revenue is a major red flag
3. Telecom boom companies used vendor financing to create the illusion of demand that didn't actually exist
4. Revenue quality analysis must examine not just timing (when recognized) but collectibility (will cash actually come)

---

## 🎓 Discussion Questions

1. How should analysts evaluate revenue when the company finances its own customers?
2. Is vendor financing always suspicious, or are there legitimate business cases?
3. How does Lucent's vendor financing compare to Sunbeam's channel stuffing?
