## 🕰️ Background

W.T. Grant was a major US retail chain that went bankrupt in 1975 — then the largest retail bankruptcy in US history. The case is legendary in accounting because Grant reported positive and growing net income for years before collapse, while its cash flow from operations was deeply negative. An analyst reading only the income statement would have seen a healthy company; an analyst reading the cash flow statement would have seen impending disaster.

---

## ❓ The Central Problem

Why did W.T. Grant's income statement and cash flow statement tell contradictory stories? The case teaches the critical lesson: net income can be manipulated or misleading, but cash flow reveals the true economic reality.

---

## 📊 Analysis

Grant aggressively expanded credit to customers (its own store credit cards), recording revenue when credit was extended — not when cash was collected. Accounts receivable ballooned as customers didn't pay. Simultaneously, Grant expanded rapidly (new stores), burning cash on inventory and build-out. The income statement showed growth (more sales, more stores). But CFO (cash from operations) was negative because: (1) Revenue was recorded but cash wasn't collected (rising AR), (2) Inventory was building (cash consumed), (3) Store expansion required capital. By the time the cash crunch became undeniable, suppliers cut credit and the company collapsed within months.

---

## 🔑 Key Lessons

1. Cash flow from operations is the most reliable measure of financial health — net income can mislead when accruals are aggressive
2. Rising accounts receivable relative to revenue is a classic warning sign — revenue is being recorded but cash isn't collected
3. Rapid expansion funded by aggressive credit extension is inherently fragile — growth masks deteriorating unit economics
4. The cash flow statement (standardized after this case contributed to SFAS 95 in 1987) exists partly because of W.T. Grant's failure
