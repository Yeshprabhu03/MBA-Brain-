# 📚 Waste Management Fraud

> **Core Lesson**: Depreciation manipulation

---

## 📋 Overview

| Attribute | Detail |

| **Core Lesson** | Depreciation manipulation |
| **Source** | HBS / Top MBA Case |

---

## 🕰️ Background

Waste Management Inc. overstated earnings by $1.7B over 5 years (1992-1997) primarily through manipulating depreciation: extending the useful lives of garbage trucks (from 8 to 12+ years) and increasing salvage values. Longer asset lives = lower annual depreciation expense = higher reported earnings. Arthur Andersen was the auditor (yet again). SEC fined Andersen $7M — the largest auditor fine at that time (2001).

---

## ❓ The Central Problem

How can something as mundane as depreciation estimates be used to commit billion-dollar fraud? The case demonstrates that accounting manipulation doesn't require complex structures — simple changes to estimates and assumptions can materially inflate earnings over time.

---

## 📊 Analysis

The straightforward but effective mechanism: extending truck useful life from 8 years to 12 years reduces annual depreciation by 33%. Across thousands of trucks and dumpsters, this reduced expenses by hundreds of millions per year. Additionally, Waste Management assigned unrealistically high salvage values to fully depreciated assets, further reducing depreciation. The fraud was unsophisticated — it just required willingness to override technical accounting staff's objections. Arthur Andersen identified the misstatements in internal memos but decided they were 'immaterial' individually even though they were clearly material in aggregate.

---

## 🔑 Key Lessons

1. Depreciation manipulation is one of the simplest forms of earnings management — changing an estimate, not fabricating transactions
2. Auditors have a duty to aggregate individually immaterial misstatements — Andersen's 'each one is small' defense was rejected
3. Management override of internal controls is the hardest fraud to detect and the most common in financial statement fraud
4. Non-cash manipulations (depreciation, amortization) are harder for analysts to detect because they don't affect cash flow

---

## 🎓 Discussion Questions

1. How would an analyst detect depreciation manipulation from public financial statements?
2. Is changing depreciation estimates always fraud, or is there legitimate management judgment involved?
3. What does Arthur Andersen's repeated failures across clients (Enron, WorldCom, Sunbeam, Waste Management) tell us about systemic audit problems?
