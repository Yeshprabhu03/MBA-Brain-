# 📚 Lehman Brothers Repo 105

> **Core Lesson**: Window dressing, ethics

## 🕰️ Background

Lehman Brothers used 'Repo 105' transactions to temporarily remove $50B from its balance sheet at each quarter-end, making its leverage ratio appear lower than reality. A Repo 105 was a repurchase agreement where Lehman pledged assets worth 105% of the cash received, allowing it to be classified as a 'sale' rather than a 'loan' under accounting rules — even though Lehman repurchased the assets days later.

---

## ❓ The Central Problem

How did Lehman use legitimate accounting rules to deceive investors about its true leverage? Repo 105 was technically legal under UK GAAP (Lehman routed transactions through its London subsidiary specifically because US GAAP wouldn't allow the classification) but fundamentally misleading. At 30:1 actual leverage, even the appearance of lower leverage was material to investors.

---

## 📊 Analysis

The mechanism: (1) Before quarter-end, Lehman 'sold' $50B in assets via Repo 105 agreements, (2) Cash received was used to pay down short-term debt, (3) At quarter-end, the balance sheet showed lower assets AND lower liabilities — reducing reported leverage, (4) Days after quarter-end, Lehman repurchased the same assets. The net economic effect was zero — but the quarterly snapshot looked better. Ernst & Young (auditor) was aware of the practice. No US law firm would provide a legal opinion supporting the classification — Lehman used UK firm Linklaters under UK GAAP.

---

## 🔑 Key Lessons

1. Window dressing (quarter-end cosmetic transactions) can mislead investors about a company's true financial condition
2. Accounting classification (sale vs. loan) has enormous balance sheet implications — Repo 105 exploited the boundary
3. Jurisdiction shopping for accounting opinions (US → UK) is a red flag indicating the treatment is aggressive
4. Auditor awareness without objection is a governance failure — Ernst & Young knew but didn't flag the misleading effect

## 🎓 Discussion Questions

1. Was Repo 105 fraud, or was it a legitimate use of accounting rules? Where is the line?
2. What red flags would have alerted an analyst to Lehman's quarter-end balance sheet manipulation?
3. How does Repo 105 compare to Enron's SPEs? Same principle or different mechanism?
