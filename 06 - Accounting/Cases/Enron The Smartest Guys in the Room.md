## 🕰️ Background

Enron used mark-to-market accounting to record expected future profits from long-term contracts immediately at signing, and created hundreds of Special Purpose Entities (SPEs) managed by CFO Andy Fastow to hide $1B+ in losses off the balance sheet. Arthur Andersen served as both auditor and consultant, earning $52M/year. Enron collapsed in December 2001 — revenue had been $111B the prior year.

---

## ❓ The Central Problem

How did Enron's accounting practices inflate earnings for years without detection? The case examines: (1) Mark-to-market abuse — booking 20 years of projected profits at contract signing, (2) SPEs — off-balance-sheet vehicles that hid losses and enriched Fastow personally ($30M+), (3) Auditor conflicts — Arthur Andersen's dual role compromised independence.

---

## 📊 Analysis

The accounting fraud operated at multiple levels: (1) Revenue: Mark-to-market let Enron book massive 'profits' from energy contracts based on optimistic projections that were never verified against reality. (2) Expenses/losses: When projections proved wrong, losses were transferred to SPEs (LJM, LJM2, Chewco) that were technically independent but actually controlled by Fastow. (3) The board waived its own conflict-of-interest rules to allow Fastow to run the SPEs while remaining CFO. (4) Arthur Andersen reviewed and approved all structures, shredded audit documents after SEC investigation began. Sarbanes-Oxley Act (2002) was passed in direct response.

---

## 🔑 Key Lessons

1. Mark-to-market requires honest estimation — without verification mechanisms, it becomes a tool for earnings fabrication
2. Off-balance-sheet structures (SPEs) can hide debt and losses from investors — post-Enron rules require consolidation of controlled entities
3. Auditor independence is structural, not personal — when audit and consulting fees from one client exceed $50M, objectivity is compromised
4. SOX reforms (CEO/CFO certification, audit committee independence, whistleblower protection) directly addressed Enron's specific failures

---

## 🎓 Discussion Questions

1. Could Enron's fraud have been detected earlier? What analytical tools would have revealed the discrepancies?
2. Was Arthur Andersen equally culpable, or was it a victim of Enron's deception?
3. Did Sarbanes-Oxley adequately address the structural problems Enron exposed?
