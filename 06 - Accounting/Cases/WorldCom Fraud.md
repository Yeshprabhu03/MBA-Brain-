# 📚 WorldCom Fraud

> **Core Lesson**: How capitalizing operating expenses ($3.8B) inflated earnings and hid the largest accounting fraud in US history — and why cash flow analysis would have revealed it years earlier.

---

## 📋 Case Overview

| Attribute | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Company** | WorldCom Inc. (later MCI) |
| **CEO** | Bernie Ebbers |
| **CFO** | Scott Sullivan (convicted) |
| **Revenue (2001)** | $35 billion — 2nd largest US telecom |
| **Fraud Amount** | ~$11 billion in capitalized expenses |
| **Bankruptcy** | July 2002 — largest in US history at that time |

---

## 🕰️ Background

WorldCom grew in the 1990s through aggressive acquisitions, becoming the second-largest US long-distance carrier. CEO Bernie Ebbers built an empire by buying 70+ companies (including MCI for $37B in 1998). Wall Street rewarded growth: WorldCom stock soared from $3 to $64 between 1990 and 1999.

When the telecom bubble burst in 2000–2001, WorldCom faced plummeting revenue. "Line costs" — fees paid to other telecoms to use their networks — were rising as a percentage of revenue, threatening to expose that WorldCom's profit margins were fictitious.

---

## ❓ The Central Problem / Fraud Mechanism

**The accounting trick**: WorldCom reclassified **operating expenses** (which hit the income statement immediately) as **capital expenditures** (which are spread over years via depreciation).

```
FRAUDULENT TREATMENT:
Line costs (operating expense) → Booked as "property, plant & equipment"
→ Not expensed immediately → Earnings look higher
→ Small depreciation charge appears over many years instead

CORRECT TREATMENT:
Line costs → Expensed immediately → Earnings lower → True picture
```

**Impact**: Between Q1 2001 and Q1 2002:
- $3.8B in line costs moved from expenses to capital equipment on the balance sheet
- Net income overstated by $3.8B (pre-tax)
- Eventually total fraud reached ~$11B

**Who knew**: CFO Scott Sullivan directed the entries. Controller David Myers executed them. Internal audit manager Cynthia Cooper discovered the fraud and reported to the audit committee.

---

## 📊 Why It Wasn't Caught Sooner

| Oversight Layer | Failure |
|----------------|---------|
| Internal Finance | CFO directed the fraud; controller complied under pressure |
| Internal Audit | Restricted from reviewing certain accounts by CFO |
| External Auditor (Arthur Andersen) | Relied on management representations; didn't verify |
| Board Audit Committee | Not briefed; CFO bypassed them |
| Analysts | Focused on revenue trends; didn't reconcile capex to assets |
| SEC | Resource constraints; telecom sector deregulatory focus |

**The signal that should have been caught**: 
- Free cash flow was persistently negative despite positive net income → impossible if real
- Capex was growing faster than any physical infrastructure programs could explain
- "Days payable" for network costs dropped → inconsistent with "billing relationship" story

---

## 🔑 Key Lessons

1. **Cash flow doesn't lie** — Net income can be manipulated; FCF is much harder to fake. WorldCom had positive net income and negative FCF for years.
2. **Capitalization vs. expensing is a judgment call — and a fraud vector** — Any "borderline" classification between opex and capex should trigger scrutiny
3. **Audit independence must be structural** — Arthur Andersen's $50M+ in WorldCom fees compromised independence; SOX separated audit and consulting
4. **Internal whistleblowers need protection** — Cynthia Cooper reported despite retaliation risk; post-WorldCom SOX created federal whistleblower protections
5. **The CFO controls the numbers** — Tone at the top of finance determines whether fraud is possible
