## 🕰️ Background

Uber's surge pricing (dynamic pricing that multiplies fares during high-demand periods) is the most visible real-world application of supply-demand equilibrium. During rain, rush hour, holidays, or emergencies, prices rise 1.5-8x to (1) incentivize more drivers onto the road and (2) reduce demand from price-sensitive riders. Surge pricing is simultaneously praised by economists and hated by consumers.

---

## ❓ The Central Problem

Is dynamic pricing efficient, fair, or exploitative? Economists argue surge pricing is the optimal mechanism for clearing a market with fluctuating supply and demand. Consumers and regulators argue it's price gouging — especially during emergencies (New Year's Eve, snowstorms, Hurricane Sandy).

---

## 📊 Analysis

Economic analysis: Without surge, demand exceeds supply during peak times → long wait times (implicit cost), no incentive for new drivers → market failure. With surge: (1) Price signals attract drivers to high-demand areas — supply increases, (2) Price-sensitive riders choose alternatives (subway, walk) — demand decreases, (3) Market clears at a higher price but shorter wait time. Behavioral aspect: consumers experience loss aversion — a $50 ride that normally costs $15 feels like a $35 loss. The same consumer would pay $50 at a hotel without complaint because hotel pricing is framed differently. Uber partially retreated from extreme surge after PR backlash, introducing 'upfront pricing' that hides the multiplier.

---

## 🔑 Key Lessons

1. Dynamic pricing is economically efficient (clears the market) but psychologically painful (triggers loss aversion and fairness concerns)
2. Price elasticity of demand in ride-sharing is moderate — surge prices do redirect some riders to alternatives, proving the mechanism works
3. Supply elasticity is what makes surge valuable — higher prices bring more drivers onto the road within minutes
4. Framing matters: calling it 'busy pricing' instead of 'surge pricing' reduced consumer complaints in experiments

## 🎓 Discussion Questions

1. Is surge pricing during a natural disaster ethical? Where should the line be drawn?
2. How does surge pricing relate to the concepts of consumer surplus and deadweight loss?
3. If Uber eliminated surge pricing, what would happen to wait times and driver availability?
