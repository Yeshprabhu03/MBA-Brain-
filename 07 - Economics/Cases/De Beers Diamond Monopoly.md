## 🕰️ Background

De Beers controlled 80-90% of the global diamond supply from 1888 to the early 2000s, creating one of history's most effective monopolies. The company: (1) Stockpiled diamonds to restrict supply, (2) Invented 'A Diamond Is Forever' (1947) — creating the cultural norm of diamond engagement rings, (3) Made secondary market resale difficult to prevent used diamonds from competing with new supply.

---

## ❓ The Central Problem

How did De Beers maintain a near-perfect monopoly for 100+ years? The case examines monopoly creation, demand creation through marketing, and what ultimately eroded De Beers' market power.

---

## 📊 Analysis

De Beers' monopoly maintenance required both supply control AND demand creation: (1) Supply: Bought up new diamond mines globally; agreed with governments (Botswana, Russia) to sell exclusively through De Beers' Central Selling Organisation (CSO). (2) Demand: The 'A Diamond Is Forever' campaign is possibly the most effective marketing campaign in history — before 1938, diamond engagement rings were uncommon. De Beers created the 'two months salary' norm. (3) Anti-resale: By discouraging diamond resale (diamonds are 'forever,' not resold), De Beers prevented a secondary market from undermining new diamond prices. Erosion: (1) 2000s: Russia and Australia broke from CSO, (2) Lab-grown diamonds emerged as substitutes, (3) Antitrust pressure forced De Beers to abandon market share to ~35% today.

---

## 🔑 Key Lessons

1. Monopoly profitability requires BOTH supply control AND demand creation — De Beers did both brilliantly
2. Manufactured demand (diamond engagement ring norm) can be more powerful than natural demand — De Beers created a $80B+ market through marketing
3. Monopolies are inherently unstable — new supply sources, substitutes (lab-grown), and regulation eventually erode market power
4. Preventing secondary markets (resale) is a key monopoly maintenance strategy — if consumers resold diamonds, prices would collapse

---

## 🎓 Discussion Questions

1. Was De Beers' monopoly beneficial or harmful to consumers?
2. How does the diamond market illustrate the concept of artificial scarcity?
3. Will lab-grown diamonds ultimately destroy the natural diamond market?
