# ⚖️ Supply and Demand

> **Definition**: The foundational model of market economics describing how the price and quantity of goods are determined through the interaction of buyers (demand) and sellers (supply). The market-clearing price is where supply equals demand.

*Foundation of nearly all economic analysis — both micro and macro.*

---

## 📈 Demand

**The Law of Demand**: As price increases, quantity demanded decreases — all else equal.

$$Q_D = f(P, \text{Income}, P_{substitutes}, P_{complements}, \text{Preferences}, \text{Expectations})$$

### The Demand Curve

```
Price
  │\
  │  \
  │    \          (Downward sloping)
  │      \
  │        \
  └──────────────→ Quantity
```

### Shifts in Demand (moving the curve)
The demand *curve* shifts (not movement along) when:

| Shift Factor | How Demand Changes |
|-------------|-------------------|
| Income rises (normal good) | Demand increases → curve shifts right |
| Income rises (inferior good) | Demand decreases → curve shifts left |
| Substitute's price rises | Demand increases (e.g., Pepsi up → Coke demand up) |
| Complement's price rises | Demand decreases (e.g., gas prices up → SUV demand falls) |
| Consumer preferences shift | Depends on direction |
| Positive future expectations | Demand increases now |

---

## 📉 Supply

**The Law of Supply**: As price increases, quantity supplied increases — all else equal.

$$Q_S = f(P, \text{Input Costs}, \text{Technology}, \text{Number of Sellers}, \text{Government Policy})$$

### The Supply Curve

```
Price
  │          /
  │        /
  │      /          (Upward sloping)
  │    /
  │  /
  └──────────────→ Quantity
```

### Shifts in Supply
| Shift Factor | How Supply Changes |
|-------------|-------------------|
| Input costs fall | Supply increases → curve shifts right |
| Technology improves | Supply increases |
| More sellers enter | Supply increases |
| Government subsidy | Supply increases |
| Government tax/regulation | Supply decreases |

---

## ⚖️ Market Equilibrium

**Equilibrium**: Where supply = demand. The price that clears the market.

```
Price
  │    S
  │   /\
  │  /  \
P*│ /    \← Equilibrium
  │/  D   \
  └──────────→ Quantity
       Q*
```

**At prices above P\***: Surplus (supply > demand) → price falls toward equilibrium
**At prices below P\***: Shortage (demand > supply) → price rises toward equilibrium

**Price mechanism**: Markets are self-correcting through price signals.

## 🎯 Business Applications

### Price Elasticity

$$E_d = \frac{\% \Delta Q_D}{\% \Delta P}$$

| |E| | Type | Business Implication |
|---|------|---------------------|
| > 1 | Elastic | Lower price → more revenue |
| = 1 | Unit elastic | Revenue unchanged |
| < 1 | Inelastic | Raise price → more revenue |

**Factors creating inelasticity**: Few substitutes, necessities, small budget share, addiction, high switching costs.

### Consumer and Producer Surplus

```
Price
  │\
  │  \
  │ CS \← Consumer Surplus (value captured by buyers)
P*│────.\────
  │  PS .\← Producer Surplus (value captured by sellers)
  │        \
  └──────────→ Quantity
              Q*
CS = buyer's value - price paid
PS = price received - seller's cost
Total Surplus (Social Welfare) = CS + PS
```

**Policy implication**: Price controls (ceilings/floors) reduce total surplus → deadweight loss.

## 🏭 Market Structures

| Structure | # Sellers | Product | Price Control | Example |
|-----------|----------|---------|--------------|---------|
| **Perfect Competition** | Many | Homogeneous | None (price taker) | Wheat, gold |
| **Monopolistic Competition** | Many | Differentiated | Some | Restaurants, clothing |
| **Oligopoly** | Few | Homogeneous or differentiated | Significant | Airlines, autos, tech |
| **Monopoly** | One | Unique | Full (price setter) | Utilities, OS (historically) |

**Oligopoly** is most strategically interesting — [[Game Theory]] applies here.
