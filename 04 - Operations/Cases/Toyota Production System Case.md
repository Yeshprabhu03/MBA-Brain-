# 📚 Toyota Production System (TPS)

> **Core Lesson**: Lean manufacturing and Kaizen — how Toyota built the world's most efficient production system by relentlessly eliminating waste and empowering frontline workers to solve problems.

*The most-studied operations case in the world. Basis for Lean Manufacturing, Six Sigma, and Lean Startup.*

---

## 📋 Case Overview

| Attribute | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Company** | Toyota Motor Corporation |
| **Period** | 1950s–1980s (development) |
| **Leaders** | Taiichi Ohno, Shigeo Shingo, Eiji Toyoda |
| **Context** | Post-WWII Japan: scarce capital, small market, need to compete with US giants |
| **Core Question** | How can a small, resource-constrained company beat Ford and GM? |

---

## 🕰️ Background: The Problem

**1950, Japan**: Toyota was tiny and nearly bankrupt. The US auto industry (Ford, GM) dominated through:
- Mass production (huge volumes)
- Economies of scale (long production runs)
- Large inventories as buffer stock
- Specialized workers doing repetitive tasks

Toyota **could not** compete on this model — no capital, small market, scarce materials.

**Taiichi Ohno's insight**: Rather than copy Ford, do the opposite.

---

## 🔑 The TPS Philosophy

### The Two Pillars
1. **Jidoka** (Autonomation): Machines stop automatically when a defect occurs. Humans aren't assigned to monitor machines — they're free to add value. Quality is built in, not inspected at the end.

2. **Just-in-Time (JIT)**: Produce only what is needed, when it is needed, in the amount needed. Eliminates all inventory buffers → forces problem-solving.

**The counterintuitive logic**: Buffers and inventory *hide* problems. Toyota deliberately removes buffers to *expose* problems → fix them → improve the system.

---

## 🗑️ The Seven Wastes (Muda)

Ohno catalogued all non-value-adding activities:

| Waste | Toyota Example | Fix |
|-------|---------------|-----|
| Overproduction | Building cars nobody ordered | JIT — build to order |
| Waiting | Workers idle waiting for parts | Flow, Kanban |
| Transportation | Moving parts across plant | Cellular layout |
| Over-processing | More quality than needed | Standard work |
| Inventory | Safety stock hiding problems | Reduce lot sizes |
| Motion | Workers walking to get tools | 5S, workstation design |
| Defects | Scrapping or reworking parts | Jidoka, Poka-yoke |

---

## 🔄 Kaizen: Continuous Improvement

**Kaizen** (改善) = "change for better" = small, incremental improvements by everyone, every day.

**How Toyota operationalizes it**:
- Every worker is authorized to stop the production line (Andon cord)
- When the line stops, the **whole team** swarms the problem
- Fix the root cause, not the symptom (5 Whys)
- Document the improvement as new standard work
- Never return to the old way

**Result**: Toyota workers filed 1 million improvement suggestions per year in the 1980s — 97% implemented.

---

## 📊 TPS vs. Mass Production

| Dimension | Mass Production (GM/Ford) | Toyota Production System |
|-----------|--------------------------|-------------------------|
| Production trigger | Sales forecast (push) | Customer order (pull) |
| Lot size | Large (economies of scale) | Small (flexibility) |
| Inventory | Weeks of buffer stock | Hours or less |
| Quality | Inspect at end (QC) | Built in (Jidoka) |
| Defect rate | High (fix later) | Near-zero |
| Worker role | Specialized, monotonous | Multi-skilled, problem-solving |
| Layout | Departmental | Cellular (U-shaped)|
| Setup time | Hours (avoid changeovers) | Minutes (SMED) |

---

## 📈 Outcomes: Why It Won

By 1980s, Toyota showed:
- **Defect rate**: 45 defects per 100 cars (Toyota) vs. 135 (US average)
- **Inventory**: 2 hours of parts (Toyota) vs. 2 weeks (GM)
- **Productivity**: Toyota plant = 2× labor productivity of GM
- **Development time**: Toyota new model in 3.5 years vs. 5+ years (US/Europe)

1984: NUMMI joint venture — Toyota took over a shuttered GM plant with the same workers. In 18 months, it became GM's best quality plant. **The system, not the workforce, was the difference.**

## 🌍 TPS Beyond Manufacturing

The TPS spawned entire industries:
- **Lean Manufacturing** (Womack/Jones "The Machine That Changed the World")
- **Six Sigma** — quality focus; complementary
- **Lean Healthcare** — Virginia Mason, ThedaCare
- **Agile/Scrum** — software development borrows from TPS
- **Lean Startup** — Eric Ries's explicit homage to TPS

---

## 🔑 Key Lessons

1. **Constraints reveal problems** — Remove buffers to expose and fix root causes
2. **Pull, don't push** — Build to demand, not forecast
3. **Quality at source** — Stop and fix vs. inspect and repair
4. **Workers are problem-solvers** — Engage everyone's intellect, not just their hands
5. **Small lots + fast changeovers** = flexibility without sacrificing efficiency
6. **Systems thinking** — TPS is a coherent system; borrowing pieces without the philosophy fails

## 🎓 Discussion Questions

1. Why does removing inventory buffers *improve* quality rather than harm production?
2. What would have happened if Toyota had tried to compete with Ford on Ford's terms?
3. How does the Andon cord system change the role of frontline workers vs. managers?
4. Why did GM struggle to adopt TPS principles even after seeing them work at NUMMI?
5. What are the limits of TPS — where does it NOT apply?
