# 📚 Kodak Digital Failure

> **Core Lesson**: Innovation blindness, incumbents

## 🕰️ Background

Kodak invented the digital camera in 1975 (Steve Sasson, engineer) but chose not to commercialize it because digital would cannibalize its enormously profitable film business. Film had 70%+ gross margins and Kodak held 90% US market share. By 2000 digital cameras were mainstream; by 2012 Kodak filed for bankruptcy.

---

## ❓ The Central Problem

Why do incumbent firms systematically fail to adopt the very innovations they invent? Kodak faced the classic innovator's dilemma: digital cannibalizes film. Film generated $10B+ in revenue with massive margins. Digital cameras were initially inferior (low resolution, expensive). Management chose to protect existing profits rather than invest in the future.

---

## 📊 Analysis

Kodak's leadership recognized digital was coming but consistently chose incremental responses: digital kiosks in stores, digital photo printing, half-hearted digital camera lines. The core problem was organizational: Kodak's culture, incentives, supply chain, and entire business model were built around film chemistry. Every dollar invested in digital was a dollar that undermined film. By the time Kodak committed fully to digital (~2003), it was 5+ years behind Canon, Sony, and Nikon. Then smartphones eliminated standalone digital cameras entirely.

---

## 🔑 Key Lessons

1. Incumbents can invent disruptive technology but fail to commercialize it when it threatens existing profit pools
2. The innovator's dilemma is structural, not about intelligence — Kodak's leaders were smart; the incentive system was the problem
3. Cannibalization by yourself is better than cannibalization by competitors — if you don't disrupt your own business, someone else will
4. Disruption often comes from below: inferior-but-cheaper products that improve over time

---

## 🎓 Discussion Questions

1. Was there a realistic strategy that would have let Kodak survive the transition to digital?
2. How does Kodak's failure compare to Fujifilm's survival? (Fujifilm diversified into chemicals, healthcare, cosmetics)
3. What organizational changes would have been needed for Kodak to pursue digital aggressively?
