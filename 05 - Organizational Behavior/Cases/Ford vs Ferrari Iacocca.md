## 🕰️ Background

Lee Iacocca's career spans two of the most dramatic power struggles in corporate history: (1) At Ford (1960s-1978), he rose from engineer to president, championed the Mustang and Pinto, but was fired by Henry Ford II despite exceptional performance. (2) At Chrysler (1978-1992), he saved the company from bankruptcy, secured a controversial $1.5B government loan guarantee, and launched the minivan.

---

## ❓ The Central Problem

How do organizational politics, founder power, and personal ego shape executive careers? Iacocca was arguably the best automotive executive of his generation — but power dynamics, not performance, determined his fate at Ford.

---

## 📊 Analysis

At Ford: Henry Ford II fired Iacocca in 1978 despite the company earning record profits. The reason was personal — Ford feared Iacocca's growing power, charisma, and political connections (Iacocca was considering running for President). 'Sometimes you just don't like somebody,' Ford said. At Chrysler: Iacocca negotiated the first major US corporate bailout ($1.5B in government loan guarantees), cut his own salary to $1/year as a symbol, launched the K-car platform and minivan, and repaid the loans 7 years early. His autobiography became the best-selling non-fiction book of the 1980s.

---

## 🔑 Key Lessons

1. Performance alone doesn't protect executives — organizational politics and personal relationships with power holders matter enormously
2. Founders (or founding families) wield power that transcends formal governance structures — Henry Ford II overrode the board
3. Crisis creates opportunity for transformational leadership — Iacocca's greatest achievements came from Chrysler's near-death
4. Symbolic leadership (the $1 salary) builds public and employee trust during crises

---

## 🎓 Discussion Questions

1. Was Henry Ford II right to fire Iacocca? What does this tell us about CEO-board dynamics in family-controlled companies?
2. How did Iacocca's leadership style change between Ford (political player) and Chrysler (turnaround CEO)?
3. Should the US government have bailed out Chrysler in 1979? What precedent did it set?
