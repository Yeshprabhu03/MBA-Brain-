## 🕰️ Background

When Lou Gerstner became IBM CEO in 1993, the company was losing $8B/year, market cap had fallen 75%, and industry consensus was that IBM should be broken into separate companies. Gerstner — the first outsider CEO — rejected breakup, refocused on enterprise services and solutions, shifted from hardware to software/services, and restored IBM to profitability within 18 months.

---

## ❓ The Central Problem

How does an outsider CEO transform an entrenched, bureaucratic culture? IBM's culture ('Big Blue') was legendary for its rigidity: white shirts, hierarchical decision-making, internal focus, and geographic fiefdoms that competed against each other rather than external competitors.

---

## 📊 Analysis

Gerstner's key moves: (1) Kept IBM integrated — rejected the McKinsey/board advice to break up. His insight: customers wanted integrated solutions, not component vendors. (2) Shifted from products to services — IBM Global Services became the largest IT services business globally. (3) Cultural revolution: eliminated the dress code (symbolic), forced cross-unit collaboration, customer-first orientation. (4) Embraced the internet early (1994-1995) — repositioned IBM as the enterprise internet infrastructure company. His book 'Who Says Elephants Can't Dance?' documented the transformation.

---

## 🔑 Key Lessons

1. Cultural transformation requires symbolic acts (dress code) AND structural changes (reorganizing around customers, not products)
2. Outsider CEOs can challenge assumptions that insiders can't see — Gerstner questioned 'break up IBM' because he thought like a customer
3. Integration can be a strategic advantage — keeping IBM together let it sell solutions competitors couldn't offer
4. Services businesses are more resilient than hardware businesses — IBM's pivot from products to services saved the company
