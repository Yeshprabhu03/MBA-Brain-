## 🕰️ Background

Elizabeth Holmes founded Theranos at 19 (2003), promising to revolutionize blood testing with a device that could run hundreds of tests from a single finger prick. She raised $700M+ at a $9B peak valuation, assembled a board of luminaries (Henry Kissinger, George Shultz, Jim Mattis), and was celebrated as the youngest female self-made billionaire. The technology never worked. Holmes was convicted of fraud in 2022.

---

## ❓ The Central Problem

How did a founder with no working product raise $700M and maintain a $9B valuation for over a decade? The case examines founder mythology, confirmation bias among board members and investors, and how charisma and narrative can substitute for substance for dangerously long periods.

---

## 📊 Analysis

Holmes cultivated a Steve Jobs persona (black turtleneck, deep voice, secrecy about the technology) that resonated with investors accustomed to trusting visionary founders. The board had zero medical or technology expertise — Kissinger, Shultz, and Mattis were national security figures selected for credibility, not competence. Internal whistleblowers (Tyler Shultz, Erika Cheung) were bullied by Theranos' legal team (David Boies). The company ran patient tests on commercial devices (Siemens) while pretending to use its own technology. Walgreens partnership was signed without technical due diligence.

---

## 🔑 Key Lessons

1. Founder charisma without technical validation is dangerous — investors confused narrative skill with engineering capability
2. Board expertise must match the company's domain — a board of generals and statesmen couldn't evaluate blood-testing technology
3. Whistleblower suppression extends fraud duration — aggressive legal threats silenced employees for years
4. 'Fake it till you make it' has limits — in healthcare, fake technology endangers patients, not just investors

---

## 🎓 Discussion Questions

1. What governance structures would have caught Theranos' fraud earlier?
2. Why did sophisticated investors and board members fail to demand working demonstrations of the technology?
3. How does the Theranos case change how we should evaluate founder-led startups?
